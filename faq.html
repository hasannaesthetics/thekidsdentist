General & Pediatric Care

Paediatric dentists are specialists who only treat children, acting as the paediatricians of dentistry. A paediatric dentist has completed additional postgraduate training dedicated to child behavior, facial growth, and children's specific dental needs, including those with special health care needs.

We provide comprehensive oral health care solely for infants, children, and adolescents from birth up to 18 years of age.

The Kids Dentist, in alignment with the American Academy of Pediatric Dentistry and the American Academy of Pediatrics, recommends that a child visits the dentist by his/her 1st birthday. This helps establish a positive dental relationship early and prevents potential tooth decay.

Primary teeth are essential for: (1) proper chewing and eating, (2) providing space and guiding permanent teeth into the correct position, (3) normal development of the jaw bones and muscles, and (4) speech development. Front baby teeth last until 6–7 years of age, while back baby molars are not replaced until ages 10–13.

A primary concern is nursing or baby bottle tooth decay. A baby risks severe decay when nursing continuously from a bottle of milk formula, breastmilk, or juice during naps or overnight. Early dental visits allow our specialists to counsel parents on preventing these dental problems.

Dental Emergencies

🚨 Immediate Action is Critical: If your child suffers a dental injury, knowing what to do immediately can mean the difference between saving and losing a tooth. Read our emergency guides below or call us at 6235 7279.

Clean around the painful tooth thoroughly. Rinse the mouth with warm salt water to displace any trapped food. Do NOT put aspirin or pain medications directly on the aching tooth or gums. If there is facial swelling, apply a cold compress. For temporary pain relief, paracetamol (e.g., Panadol syrup) can be given. Contact a dentist as soon as possible.

Rinse the injured area immediately with warm water. Apply a cold compress to the face over the area of injury to minimize swelling. If you can find the broken tooth fragments, recover them and bring them with you. Get immediate dental attention.

Time is critical: Recover the tooth, holding it by the crown (the chewing surface) and NOT the root. Rinse it gently without scrubbing or handling it excessively. Reinsert the tooth into the socket immediately and have your child bite on a clean gauze or cloth to hold it. If you cannot reinsert it, place it in a cup of fresh cold milk or saliva. See a dentist IMMEDIATELY — ideally within 60 minutes.

Apply ice to any bruised or swollen areas. If there is bleeding, apply firm but gentle pressure with a clean cloth or gauze. If the bleeding does not stop after 15 minutes of direct pressure, or if it is severe, go to the nearest emergency medical center.
