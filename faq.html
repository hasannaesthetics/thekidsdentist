🏥 About Our Practice

What is a paediatric dentist?

+

A paediatric dentist is a dental specialist who has completed additional years of training beyond dental school to focus exclusively on the oral health of children from infancy through adolescence.

This specialized training includes child psychology, growth and development, and techniques for managing children's behavior in the dental setting. Paediatric dentists are experts in treating the unique dental needs of infants, children, and teenagers, including those with special healthcare needs.

What age group of children do you treat?

+

At The Kids Dentist, we provide specialized dental care for children of all ages, from infants through adolescence (typically birth to 18 years old).

We also have extensive experience treating children with special needs and those with complex medical conditions, regardless of age. Our team is trained to handle the unique needs of each developmental stage.

🦷 First Dental Visit

When should my child first see a dentist?

+

We recommend that your child's first dental visit should occur by their first birthday or within six months of their first tooth erupting, as advocated by the American Academy of Pediatric Dentistry.

Early dental visits are crucial for several reasons:

  • Establish a dental home and build familiarity with the dental environment
  • Allow early detection of potential problems
  • Provide parents with guidance on proper oral care for infants
  • Prevent dental anxiety by creating positive early experiences
  • Receive advice on teething, pacifier use, and dietary habits

How should I prepare my child for their first visit?

+

Parents play an important role in making the first dental visit a positive experience. Here are our recommendations:

  • Keep it positive: Tell your child the dentist will count and clean their teeth and show them how to care for their smile
  • Avoid negative words: Don't use words like "needle," "pain," "drill," or "hurt" as these can cause unnecessary fear
  • Read books: Share age-appropriate books about visiting the dentist
  • Stay calm: Children pick up on parental anxiety, so remain relaxed and confident
  • Be honest: Don't promise rewards if your child "doesn't cry" – this suggests there's something to cry about

Our team uses child-friendly language to explain procedures in a pleasant, non-frightening way.

What happens during the first visit?

+

Your child's first visit will include:

  • A complete oral examination performed by our paediatric dentists
  • Assessment of tooth development and jaw growth
  • Gentle cleaning if appropriate for the child's age and cooperation
  • Discussion of proper oral hygiene techniques
  • Guidance on nutrition, pacifier use, and habits affecting oral health
  • Answers to any questions or concerns you may have

The goal is to make your child comfortable while establishing a foundation for excellent oral health.

✨ Dental Care Questions

Why are primary (baby) teeth so important?

+

Although baby teeth are temporary, they play crucial roles in your child's development:

  • Nutrition: Healthy teeth are essential for proper chewing and digestion
  • Speech development: Teeth help children learn to speak clearly
  • Space maintenance: Baby teeth hold space for permanent teeth and guide them into proper position
  • Jaw development: Proper tooth alignment helps jaw bones develop correctly
  • Self-esteem: A healthy smile contributes to confidence and social development
  • Oral health habits: Care for baby teeth establishes good habits for life

Premature loss of baby teeth can lead to crowding of permanent teeth and may require orthodontic treatment later.

How can I prevent cavities in my child's teeth?

+

Cavity prevention involves several key practices:

  • Regular brushing: Brush twice daily with fluoride toothpaste (pea-sized amount for children under 6)
  • Flossing: Start flossing when teeth touch each other
  • Healthy diet: Limit sugary snacks and drinks; choose water over juice
  • Regular check-ups: Visit every 6 months for professional cleaning and examination
  • Fluoride treatments: Professional applications strengthen tooth enamel
  • Dental sealants: Protective coating on back teeth prevents decay in grooves
  • Avoid bottle sleeping: Never put your child to bed with a bottle of milk or juice

What should I do if my child knocks out a tooth?

+

For permanent teeth (adult teeth):

  • Find the tooth and handle it by the crown (top), not the root
  • If dirty, gently rinse with milk or saliva (never use water or soap)
  • Try to place the tooth back in the socket immediately
  • If you can't replant it, keep it moist in milk or saliva
  • Call us immediately at 6235 7279
  • Seek treatment within 30 minutes for the best chance of saving the tooth

For baby teeth:

  • Do not attempt to replant baby teeth (can damage permanent tooth underneath)
  • Control bleeding with clean gauze
  • Call us for an evaluation

When will my child lose their baby teeth?

+

Children typically begin losing baby teeth around age 6, though this can vary. The process usually follows the same order as tooth eruption, with lower front teeth typically falling out first.

The timeline generally follows this pattern:

  • Ages 6-7: Lower and upper front teeth (incisors)
  • Ages 9-11: First molars and canines
  • Ages 10-12: Second molars

Most children have all their permanent teeth (except wisdom teeth) by age 12-13. If teeth are significantly early or late, we can assess whether intervention is needed.

🔧 Treatment Questions

Does my child need X-rays?

+

Dental X-rays are an important diagnostic tool that help us:

  • Detect cavities between teeth that can't be seen visually
  • Monitor the development of permanent teeth
  • Assess jaw development and tooth positioning
  • Identify infections or abscesses
  • Plan orthodontic treatment

We use digital X-rays that emit significantly less radiation than traditional X-rays. The amount of radiation exposure is minimal and considered very safe. We only take X-rays when clinically necessary and follow strict guidelines to minimize exposure.

What sedation options do you offer?

+

We offer several sedation options to ensure your child's comfort:

  • Nitrous Oxide (Laughing Gas): Mild sedative that helps children relax while remaining conscious. Effects wear off quickly after treatment.
  • Oral Sedation: Medication given by mouth before the appointment to reduce anxiety and increase cooperation.
  • General Anesthesia (GA): Complete sedation in a hospital setting for children requiring extensive treatment or those with severe anxiety or special needs.

Our dentists will discuss which option is most appropriate for your child's needs and answer all your questions about safety and procedures.

My child is afraid of the dentist. Can you help?

+

Absolutely! Managing dental anxiety is one of our specialties. As pediatric dentists, we're trained in child psychology and behavior management techniques.

We help anxious children through:

  • Creating a warm, welcoming, child-friendly environment
  • Using age-appropriate language to explain procedures
  • Building trust gradually – we never force treatment
  • Positive reinforcement and encouragement
  • Distraction techniques and comfort measures
  • Sedation options when appropriate
  • Parent coaching to avoid anxiety-inducing language

Many children who were initially fearful become enthusiastic about dental visits after experiencing our gentle, patient approach.
